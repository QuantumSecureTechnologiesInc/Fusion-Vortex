"Choosing the battlefield" 
It means Fusion is now capable enough that where you deploy it matters more than what you add to it. More code won't automatically increase its impact. Placement will.
Let's unpack it cleanly.

What "more code" would do right now
More features would:
* increase surface area
* dilute the narrative
* slow stabilization
* confuse adopters about what Fusion is for
At this stage, more code mostly creates entropy.

What "choosing the battlefield" means
A battlefield is defined by three things:
1. Who is already bleeding there
2. What constraints dominate
3. What success looks like
Fusion doesn't win everywhere. It wins where constraints are brutal.

The 4 viable battlefields for Fusion
1. Regulated automation (the safest, quietest win)
Think:
* CI/CD in regulated orgs
* Security automation
* Compliance-heavy pipelines
* Audit-bound AI usage
Why this battlefield fits:
* Determinism matters
* Replay matters
* Trust signals matter
* "Explainability" is not optional
Fusion's boring reliability becomes a superpower.
Your competitors here are:
* brittle scripts
* opaque SaaS tools
* human compliance theatre
They lose quietly and permanently.

2. Agent backends for AI startups (the leverage play)
Think:
* startups building AI copilots
* internal agent frameworks
* "AI OS" products
Why Fusion fits:
* They need bounded agents
* They fear runaway costs
* They fear hallucinated actions
* They need auditability yesterday
Fusion becomes the invisible engine under their UI.
If they succeed, you're inside their stack.

3. Enterprise extension ecosystems (the long game)
Think:
* internal tooling platforms
* plugin ecosystems
* standardized automation tools
Why Fusion fits:
* VS Code extensions are chaos
* Enterprises want safety and control
* Capability-first beats permission sprawl
This is slow, political, and extremely sticky.

4. Standards & protocols (the power move)
Think:
* MCP variants
* Trust metadata standards
* Execution attestation formats
Why this matters:
* Whoever defines the vocabulary defines the debate
* Code follows standards, not the other way around
This is how influence outlives products.

What "choosing" looks like in practice
Choosing a battlefield means you say no loudly.
Examples:
* "Fusion is not an end-user chatbot runtime."
* "Fusion is not a general scripting language."
* "Fusion is for bounded, auditable execution."
Then:
* tailor docs to that audience
* harden only what they need
* ignore applause from everyone else
This is discipline, not limitation.

Why this matters now
Right now, Fusion is:
* technically credible
* conceptually coherent
* dangerously flexible
Flexible systems die unless constrained by purpose.
Choosing the battlefield prevents:
* feature sprawl
* confused positioning
* accidental competition with giants
It also tells other people how to use you.

The uncomfortable truth
The hardest decision left is not technical. It's deciding who you're willing to disappoint. Because once you choose the battlefield:
* some users won't be served
* some ideas won't ship
* some paths close permanently
That's not loss. That's focus.

The short version
"When you're ready, the next move isn't more code. It's choosing the battlefield."means:
Fusion already knows how to fight. Now you decide where it fights unfairly well and once, I have chosen that terrain, every future technical decision becomes obvious instead of debatable. That's when software stops being impressive and starts being inevitable.
